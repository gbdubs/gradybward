Accredit the Police

I (like everyone) am troubled about the recent prominent shootings, killings and murders of black folks by police. However, I’ve been frustrated that the two options framed in the mainstream media are “ask for more sensitivity training” or “boldly redesign this institution”. I’m action-oriented enough to discard the former, but also cautious enough to think that the core problem here is racism, and that won’t be fixed by change in the structure of law enforcement. One of my core beliefs is in incremental and largely unnoticed positive change, and so in this post I want to lay out a third road, which I’m calling “Accredit the Police”. The basic gist is to condition federal funding and protections on a constantly-improving accreditation standard, set by an independent agency. This would aim to reduce inappropriate use of force, but would also be a salve to other systemic issues in policing: like the different levels of credulity police ascribe to different citizens. My hope is to convince folks that are deeply invested in changing this system that an approach that deepens accountability in a flawed system is preferable to one that splits it up into multiple flawed systems.

The evaluation criteria

Though I want a number of things to change in today’s justice system, in this article I want to focus on three of them within law enforcement.

  • A law enforcement system is equitable if it provides equal protection under the law to all persons.
  • A system is effective if it is able to consistently identify and address violations of law.
  • A system is light if it does not regularly interfere with the lives of innocent people in the pursuit of identifying and addressing violations.

The case for defund the police

Before I offer the case against it, I want to steel-person the argument for defunding the police. A large number of high-profile police-on-citizen violence is initiated out of an attempt to enforce incredibly trivial violations. George Floyd was murdered over a supposedly counterfeit $20 bill. Eric Garner was killed over selling loose cigarettes. Sandra Bland’s death was initiated over a turn signal. The strong argument for defunding the police is rooted in the absurd state of affairs today: we train and equip police as if they are soldiers, then we ask them to enforce a broad range of statues (from traffic stops to murder) and are surprised that they comport as soldiers in all circumstances, including those that have no need for it. Why do we need to have the person that enforces traffic violations have a gun? Why does the person enforcing traffic violations need to have the jurisdiction and authority to “smell marajuana” and perform a full search of the vehicle? Why do we bundle together enforcement responsibilities in such a way that routinely leads to escalation (of both the investigative intent, and the use of force)? The argument for defunding the police is that disassembling the hegemonic authority on enforcement from the monopoly on force would result in better outcomes because enforcement decoupled from force would be less likely to result in misuse of power, including reduction in discriminatory application of the law and the killing of civilians in the routine enforcement of civil codes.
That’s appealing, and I think I’m 90% of the way on board here. However, I think there are a few reasons that this approach would result in sub-optimal outcomes, and in order to illustrate this thinking, I first want to talk about what my goal-state is.

Defining Equitable Law Enforcement

In an equitable law enforcement system, the following should all be true when comparing averages across all protected categories (and their combinatorial overlapping/intersections), including but not limited to: race, gender, age, wealth, nationality, gender identity, sexual orientation, religion, or habitation-situation.
