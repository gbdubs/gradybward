Curatorial Vocabulary

In disagreements around the appropriate use of and justified restriction on technology (tech-use), you will rarely find compromise or gradation. I think this is because we lack a nuanced vocabulary to grapple with tech-use. Unlike other types ethical questions, tech-use doesn’t have a storied history of thought experiments, historical error and fallacies to guide our reasoning. In addition, reducing tech-use questions to moral ones typically involves lots of assumptions, examples and hand waving, and with that much subjectivity, the conclusion is likely more indicative of the chosen translation than of an actual moral parallel. Because we lack this vocabulary, debates around tech-use tend to fall back to political memes that are capable of crystalizing the situation, engendering strong feelings without actually illuminating the interplay of competing values. I imagine that the next few decades (in both the public sphere and my professional life) will require a good deal of navigating questions of tech-use, so I figured it might be a good exercise to see if I can develop some of that vocabulary for myself.
Today I want to look at one such issue, and try to sketch out a set of terms and principles that can address it in a meaningful way. The issue I’m interested in is what types of obligations user content platforms have to curate the data on their sites. This question if crystalized through the meme of Free Speech might lead one to think that no censorship of user content should be permitted. The same question crystalized through a utilitarian framework might justify a broad set of curatorial obligations. I don’t think either of these are illuminating frames. Instead, let’s start by choosing to define some terms.

  • A platform is an entity that allows the input and retrieval of data generated by users.
  • Content is the data intentionally created by a user and uploaded to the platform.
  • An Access is a user reading, downloading or otherwise engaging with a piece of Content.
  • Visibility describes the size of the audience that can access a piece of content.
Reach describes the size of the audience that actually ends up accessing a piece of content.

    • Transmission Pattern describes a processes that result in accesses.

    • Exogenous Transmission describes organic access patterns that are outside of the capacity for the platform to control or explain (ex: accesses of content that occur directly inbound).
    • Observed Transmission describes processes for triggering accesses that is visible and explicable to the platform, but outside of it’s ability to control (ex: a user shares a piece of content directly with another user)
    • Unintentional Transmission describes a process for triggering accesses that is controlled by the platform, but is agnostic to the content and value of the item. (ex: searching within an email client “email I received yesterday”)
    • Intentional Transmission describes a process for triggering accesses that is controlled by the platform, and intended to guide the user to an outcome, using some inference about the value of the content to the user (ex: searching for “dentist” in a search engine)
    • Recommended Transmission describes a process for triggering accesses that is controlled by the platform and has the explicit objective of propagating the content (ex: a video platform that auto-plays a new video after you finish watching a video)
    • Objection-ability describes the degree to which content is objectionable. Note that we assume some roughly consistent ethical frame for evaluating content, and fully accept that this is a glaring omission. For the sake of this discussion, assume the ethical frame inclusive of all common viewpoints in western liberal democracies.

    • Harm Derived Content is content that by it’s very nature is objectionable because it’s generation requires significant and agreed upon harm. Child sexual abuse imagery and terrorist beheadding videos are the only things I can think of in this category.
    • Harm Generating Content is content that is circulated with the intent or effect of bringing clearly demonstrable harm to the consumer of the content. Examples include bullying, encouragement to self harm, financial scams, malware and health frauds.
    • Harm Inciting Content is content that encourages others to actions that are not harmful to themselves, but are harmful to others. Hate speech.
    • Age Appropriate Content is content that is not suitable for young viewers, and may not be appropriate in all spheres of life, but is not objectionable in it’s existence or consumption given an appropriate time place and manner.
    • Incorrect Content is content that makes incorrect claims (intentionally or otherwise) that is not likely to result in clearly explicable harms.
    • Sensitive Content describes content that is fully suitable for personal consumption, but that should not be shared broadly.
    • Curatorial Obligation refers to the question of “what ought a platform do in a certain situation”
