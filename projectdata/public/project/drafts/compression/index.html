Compression

A year ago I set out to better understand privacy. I read about the history of data collection, I became certified in privacy practices, I began working on privacy issues in my day-to-day work. But what I first looked for has proven to be the most elusive, a general model for thinking about issues of privacy - one that isn’t based in a given historical or ethical frame. I think I’ve started to settle on such a model, so I figured I’d write down my thoughts to help me make more sense of them.

The core transaction

As we move through the world, information about our circumstances and choices are collected, compiled, and bought and sold. This practice dates back at least a century with approximately the same roles in the “core” transaction:

  • People exist and make choices
  • Collectors record information about their existence and choices
  • Availers purchase this information for their benefit
First to dispatch an often included addition to this model: it is often posited that the person consents into this transaction, and receives benefit. We can obviously dismiss this potential addition by noting that in many cases people do not benefit. But this also misses the mark at a much more structural level. People are not the principle beneficiaries of the transaction. The transaction is ultimately mediated by, and to the principle benefit of, the availer.

  • Regardless of the mechanism, what the availer gets from this transaction is the reduction of uncertainty. Crudely, we know the value of this uncertainty reduction is larger than the one the availer pays to a collector.
  • But think about uncertainty from the perspective of the person. In a natural state, the person is the best forecaster of their future actions - an inherent personal knowledge. This knowledge is powerful, and is the basis on which the person forecasts and charts their future.
  • This transaction cedes (or, at minimum, duplicates) some of this power from the person to the availer. Availers pay collectors as a means to increase their power in the context of the person.
Once the availer has reduced uncertainty, this brings them some benefit (below), and in some cases they choose to pass part of this benefit on to the person, but it is a mistake to equate the voluntary capitulation of value to the person as a part of the core transaction. No matter how appreciable the benefits the person gets after the fact, the availer determines this reward from a place of (increased) power, and certain financial gain.

What do availers want?

Availers seek to reduce uncertainty about a person, either to predict their future or to try to shape it. - Prediction comprises estimation of the likelihood of a person’s future state (what are the odds P will have lung disease at 68?) or choices (what are the odds P attempts to quit smoking this year?) - Manipulation comprises attempts to alter a person’s future behavior (what series of messages will be most likely to help P quit smoking?)
These happen within two contexts:

  • Personal Context is the information from, and behaviors of, specific individuals
  • Aggregated Context is asking questions about a population that is sufficently large…
These distinctions are subtle, but I promise they matter. Here’s some helpful examples:

  • A mortgage creditor looking at your previous credit history in order to inform what rate they will charge you is personalized prediction.
  • A mortgage creditor looking at past records from other loans to inform their overall rates is aggregated prediction.
  • A mortgage creditor using AB testing to decide to send red letters one day after late notices is aggregated manipulation.
  • A mortgage creditor deciding how to frame delinquency to you based on information about you is personalized manipulation.
PP is as old as time. AP has been around since the sci rev. AM peaked in the 50s. PM is today and the future.
